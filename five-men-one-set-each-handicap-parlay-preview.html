Five players, two cities, one very small request: take a single set. Not the match — just one set, each. The set-handicap parlay is the insurance policy you write when you fancy five men but don’t fancy your nerves. At 2.985, fifteen euro turns into forty-four-and-change if none of the five gets swept off the court. That is the entire job.

Start where the surface is red. While the rest of this slip plays out on a London lawn, Marco Cecchinato has gone home to Milan to defend the Aspria Tennis Cup — a Challenger he has won twice, on the clay where he has spent his whole career. The former world No. 16 and 2018 Roland Garros semi-finalist — the man who once knocked Novak Djokovic out of Paris — draws a young Italian wild card, Filippo Romano, in an all-Italian opener. Cecchinato has never won a main-draw match at Wimbledon in his life; put him on red clay, though, and he becomes the teacher. For the handicap the lesson can be abridged: he need only take one set off the boy, which on his own dirt is barely a question.

Onto the grass, and the most enjoyable mismatch on the card. Tomas Martin Etcheverry is the higher-ranked man — No. 32 to Sonego’s No. 72 — and on paper that should settle it. On a lawn it settles nothing. Etcheverry is a clay-court slider: a man with a respectable record on the dirt and a dismal 5-14 for his career on grass, who has lost both his grass matches this June and went out at Eastbourne having actually led by a set. He slides; he does not garden. Lorenzo Sonego, by contrast, owns a winning grass record, two Wimbledon fourth-round runs, and a serve-and-forehand game built for fast, low bounces — which is precisely why the bookmakers, ranking be damned, make the Italian the favourite. For +1.5 I need only that he hold the Argentine to nothing worse than a tight three-setter. The market thinks he wins it outright.

Then the serve. Giovanni Mpetshi Perricard is six-foot-eight of almost nothing but first delivery — the nearest thing this generation has to a John Isner, a man whose limitations in a rally are no secret and whose virtues arrive at 140 miles per hour. On grass, that is enough. He has held an absurd 96% of his service games on the surface this season, reached the fourth round on his Wimbledon debut, and last year took Taylor Fritz to a fifth set in this very draw. Yannick Hanfmann is a big server too, but his grass form has been a coin-flip. For the handicap I do not need Mpetshi to out-rally anyone — I need his serve to drag a single set out of the German, which on a lawn it tends to do almost by accident.

Which leaves the two errands. Mackenzie McDonald comes through qualifying, but he carries an eastern-grip forehand made for grass and a Wimbledon fourth round on his CV from 2018 — too much pedigree for a compatriot, Patrick Kypson, with none of it. And Tallon Griekspoor brings a winning career grass record and a clear price edge over James Duckworth, an honest Australian veteran whose own grass form this year reads 1-3. Two favourites, two surfaces of comfort, two opponents short of an answer. The handicap asks only that neither McDonald nor Griekspoor contrives to lose in straight sets — which, against this opposition, would take some doing.

⚡ Fun fact · the serve-bot’s tariff
Giovanni Mpetshi Perricard stands 6ft 8in and has held an extraordinary 96% of his service games on grass this season. He is the closest thing the tour has to a modern John Isner: outsource the rallies, win the tie-breaks. On a surface that prizes a free point above all else, a man who almost never loses serve almost never gets swept — which is the entire premise of his leg.
Five men, one set each, two cities, one modest ambition: that nobody gets swept. Cecchinato to school the kid on his clay, Sonego to send a clay-slider back where he came from, Mpetshi to let his serve fetch a set, and McDonald and Griekspoor to do the chores. Nothing here is heroic — which, as ever, is exactly the moment it likes to go wrong.
