01 — Insane Parlay Wins
🏆 All-Time Bookmaker Disaster
2015–16 · Premier League · Sky Bet / Ladbrokes / William Hill
Leicester City win the title at 5,000-1. Bookmakers weep. Mandy Clarke does not.
At the start of the 2015-16 season, Leicester City — who had survived relegation by the skin of their teeth the previous May — were priced at 5,000-1 to win the Premier League. ESPN noted those odds were 119 times more unlikely than Buster Douglas beating Mike Tyson. Then they went and won it.

Mandy Clarke, a medical secretary just outside Leicester, was handed a £10 ticket as a joke by a colleague. Payout: £50,000. "The money is an unbelievable amount for me. It is a life-changing amount," she told Sky Sports. The colleague who gave her the ticket presumably has a complicated relationship with Christmas.

"The unthinkable has become reality and we're facing paying out the sort of liability that you joke about at the start of the season."

— Sky Bet spokesman · May 2016 · Sky Sports ✅

"Leicester winning the title was in the realms of the ridiculous, and it has cost us the biggest ante-post payout in our 130-year history."

— Ladbrokes spokesman · May 2016 · Guardian ✅
Industry-wide liability: estimated £20m (Sky Sports) — Ladbrokes vowed never again to offer 5,000-1 on a football team to win a title.
How did I miss this one? Probably because I was busy watching Vardy score. — C.C.F.
💰 Modern Parlay God
$30.11 → $1,980,073.12. Six legs. One night. Florida.
On 24 April 2026, a Broward County bettor turned $30.11 into just under $2 million on a six-leg MLB home-run parlay. Odds: +6,576,031. Riley Greene, Coby Mayo, Jesús Sánchez, Bryce Harper, Nick Kurtz and Jazz Chisholm Jr. all had to homer. They all homered.
"One of the highest odds parlays that we've ever paid out — with only six legs! Send this ticket to Cooperstown."
— Neil Walsh, SVP Sportsbook, Hard Rock Bet · April 2026 ✅
How did I miss that? Maybe because I don't bet home runs. Maybe because $30 with those odds is the definition of "what do I have to lose." The answer, for once, was nothing. — C.C.F.

🎰 The £2 Miracle
Steve Whiteley. £2 Tote. £1.45 million. Exeter Racecourse. Free tickets.
In 2011, a 61-year-old heating engineer attended Exeter Racecourse on free promotional tickets. He couldn't afford the £32 Tote Jackpot, so placed the £2 version — one horse per race. All six landed. The last was called Lupita. Payout: £1.45 million — the largest Tote payout in British history.
He was a social gambler who only attended because friends had free tickets. He couldn't afford the proper version of the bet.
— Guardian · 2011 ✅
The horse was called Lupita. He couldn't afford the full bet. He won £1.45 million. The moral of this story is unclear and I refuse to draw one. — C.C.F.

🏌️ Tiger Woods. First bet. $1.19M.
James Adducci. $85,000. Tiger at 14-1. First sports bet of his life.
In April 2019, James Adducci — a 39-year-old self-employed daytrader from Wisconsin — flew to Las Vegas and staked $85,000 on Tiger Woods to win the Masters at 14-1. It was his first-ever sports bet. Tiger won. Adducci collected $1.19 million — the largest golf futures payout in William Hill US history at the time.
"This is a story for the ages. Tiger climbs back to the top, and a guy from Wisconsin, on his first sports bet ever, wins over $1 million betting on him."
— Joe Asher, CEO William Hill US · April 2019 ✅
He then staked $100,000 of his winnings on Tiger to win the 2019 Grand Slam at 100-1. That one did not come in. The universe balanced itself. — C.C.F.

03 — Match-Fixing: The Documented Record
The Fixing Timeline · 1919 → 2011
All entries legally documented · Convictions, bans, and eternal shame
1919
Black Sox · World Series · Chicago White Sox
Eight players conspired to throw the World Series to the Cincinnati Reds for bribes of $70,000-$100,000. Key confessions vanished from trial files. Commissioner Landis banned all eight for life the next day, despite acquittals.
"Regardless of the verdict of juries… no player that sits in conference with a bunch of crooked players and gamblers… will ever play professional baseball." — Commissioner Landis ✅
2025 update: Commissioner Manfred ruled permanent bans expire at death. Joe Jackson eligible for Hall of Fame consideration from 2028.
1989
Pete Rose · Baseball's all-time hits leader · Lifetime ban
Rose (4,256 career hits) accepted a lifetime ban after the Dowd Report documented betting on baseball — including his own Cincinnati Reds — while serving as manager. He denied it for 15 years.
"I bet on my team every night… I bet on my team to win every night because I loved my team." — Pete Rose, My Prison Without Bars, 2004 ✅
2006
Calciopoli · Italy · Juventus relegated, Serie A titles stripped
Wiretaps revealed Juventus GM Luciano Moggi running a network to influence referee appointments. Juve stripped of the 2005 title and relegated to Serie B. Fiorentina and Lazio also relegated. Moggi banned for life.
Delicious irony: Italy won the 2006 World Cup that same summer, with Buffon, Cannavaro, and Del Piero — all from implicated clubs — in the squad. Cannavaro lifted the trophy as captain. The universe has a sense of humour.
2010
Pakistan spot-fixing · Lord's Test · England
The now-defunct News of the World caught agent Mazhar Majeed predicting exactly when bowlers would deliver no-balls. Captain Salman Butt and bowlers Mohammad Asif and Mohammad Amir were jailed by a London court in 2011. Amir later rebuilt his career.
Butt: 2.5 years · Asif: 1 year · Amir: 6 months · Majeed: 2 years 8 months — London Crown Court, November 2011 ✅
2011
Daniel Köllerer · Tennis · First lifetime ban for match-fixing
The Austrian nicknamed "Crazy Dani" became the first tennis player banned for life for match-fixing — found to have made invitations to fix matches on five occasions.
"This is a giant shock." — Köllerer, Austrian radio. Court of Arbitration for Sport upheld the ban in 2012. ✅
