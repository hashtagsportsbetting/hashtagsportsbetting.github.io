⏳ ACCEPTED · PRE-MATCH · AWAITING THE FIRST BALL
Twenty-one tennis matches. Twenty-one set-handicaps. Five pounds. And one condition holding the whole thing together: not a single player on this slip may be swept off the court. Win, or merely keep it close — that is all any of them has to do, twenty-one times over.

There is method in it, of a sort. Most of these lines are favourites handed a +1.5 cushion — Cecchinato on his Milan clay, Medvedev across the net from a 37-year-old, Tiafoe, Shelton, Davidovich Fokina. For them the bet barely qualifies as one: they simply must not be bulldozed in straight sets, which heavily-favoured players rarely are. A handful run the other way — an underdog given the cushion, needing only to nick a single set off someone better. Stack twenty-one of those together and you have built a wall of near-certainties. The trouble with a wall of near-certainties is that it only takes one brick.

The safe end runs deep. Medvedev +1.5 against Marin Cilic is about as close to a free square as a Grand Slam offers; Cecchinato, defending his Milan title, need only take a set off a teenage wild card on the clay he has owned for a decade; Katerina Siniakova already beat Qinwen Zheng on this exact lawn a year ago, which makes +1.5 almost an insult to her. The nerves live at the other end — among the women’s underdogs who actually have to grab a set. Robin Montgomery against Jasmine Paolini, Tatjana Maria against Yulia Putintseva, and shakiest of all, Mananchaya Sawangkaew at 1.444: the longest price on the slip, and the likeliest brick to fall.

⚡ Fun fact · the tyranny of the long shot
Almost every leg here is a heavy favourite, most priced between 1.09 and 1.28 — the bookmaker’s way of saying ‘this will almost certainly happen’. Multiply twenty-one almost-certainties together, though, and ‘almost’ does its quiet arithmetic: the slip prices up at 29.271, which is another way of saying it has roughly a one-in-twenty-nine chance of landing. Five pounds to win a hundred and forty-six. The longer the accumulator, the less it is analysis and the more it is a lottery ticket with opinions.
So: twenty-one matches across two days, on grass and on clay, and a fiver that asks only for universal competitiveness — no bagels, no breadsticks, nobody humiliated in straights. Reasonable, match by match. Faintly deranged, all together. Let’s hope their opponents are feeling merciful.
