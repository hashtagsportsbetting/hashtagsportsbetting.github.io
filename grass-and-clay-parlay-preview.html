Three favourites, two surfaces, one simple idea: back the men who belong where they’re standing. Two of them open Wimbledon on grass; the third defends a title on Milan clay. Ten quid says all three do their job.

Daniil Medvedev sat out the clay swing the way a cat sits out a bath — redoubling his patience, letting the red dust settle, waiting for the lawns. The man does not live for much, but he lives for grass. Wimbledon has handed him a gift to open with: Marin Cilic, a veteran we respect without reservation — a 2014 US Open champion, a 2017 finalist here, a man who has not lost in the first round at SW19 since 2011. Respect, yes; fear, no. Medvedev leads the head-to-head 4-1, is 2-0 against Cilic on grass, and beat him in Den Bosch this very month. At 1.34 the market agrees the legs of a 37-year-old don’t survive five sets against this particular opponent.

Aleksandar Kovacevic, by contrast, grazes the grass — he does not feast on it. The American stumbled through the warm-up swing, lost again in Eastbourne, and arrives at the Championships, in the kind words of one preview, searching for rhythm at the worst possible moment. Across the net stands Botic van de Zandschulp, who is everything Kovacevic is not on this surface: a 2022 Wimbledon last-16 man, comfortable on a lawn, and — the detail I like most — carrying a fresh psychological edge, having come from a set down to beat Kovacevic at the Italian Open only last month. The model gives the Dutchman 61%. At 1.51 I’ll take the man who already knows how to win this exact match.

Then a change of surface, and of register. While the grass-courters carry on in London, Marco Cecchinato has gone home to Milan to defend the Aspria Tennis Cup — a Challenger he has already won twice, on the red clay where he has spent an entire career. The former world No. 16 and 2018 Roland Garros semi-finalist — the man who once put Novak Djokovic out of Paris — draws a young Italian wild card, Filippo Romano, in an all-Italian opener. I believe in the full demonstration. But in a parlay you bank the floor, and the floor here is gentle: the professor need only take a single set off the boy, which on his own clay is barely a question. A lesson, abridged.

⚡ Fun fact · the Djokovic slayer
The youngster across the net in Milan may not dwell on it, but he is facing a man who once beat Novak Djokovic at a Grand Slam — on clay, in the 2018 Roland Garros quarter-final, 13-11 in the deciding set. Cecchinato has never won a main-draw match at Wimbledon in his life. Put him on red clay, though, and the curriculum vitae does the talking.
Three men, three jobs, two surfaces. Medvedev to enjoy the grass he has been saving himself for, van de Zandschulp to expose a man out of his element, and Cecchinato to remind the kids what clay is for. Nothing here is heroic — which, as ever, is precisely the moment it tends to go wrong.
