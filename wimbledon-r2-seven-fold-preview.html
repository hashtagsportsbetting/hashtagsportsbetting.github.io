⏳ PRE-MATCH · AWAITING RESULT
Seven second-round matches, one Wednesday, ten quid. Five of these players are asked to do the obvious thing and win; two are asked only to take a single set. No heroics, no hedging against gravity — just the quiet wager that the better player, on the surface that suits them, usually gets the job done.
The five we back to win

Start with Alejandro Davidovich Fokina, the closest thing this slip has to a banker. The Spaniard arrives on a five-match winning run through the grass swing, and — the detail that matters — he beat Fabian Marozsan only last week, coming from a set down in the Mallorca semi-finals. Marozsan, for his part, has never once passed the second round at Wimbledon and carries a losing 10-12 record on grass across his whole career. Superior surface, superior form, a fresh psychological edge from the rematch: at 1.42 this is the leg I’d insure last.

Then Anna Kalinskaya over Diane Parry. The Russian holds a 6-6 record in Grand Slam second rounds, but 4-1 across the last five — a player who has found a respectable gear since the European summer began. Parry has reached the third round of a major six times, which reads better than her 6-5 mark at this exact stage and her brutal 4-18 record against top-20 opposition. One preview put it flatly: there is no real reason to expect anything but a straight-sets Kalinskaya win. At 1.49, agreed.

Brandon Nakashima is the logical favourite against Jan-Lennard Struff, whatever the German’s pedigree. Struff owns a tidy 4-1 record in Wimbledon second rounds and reached the third round here in both 2024 and 2025 — but he has not strung two main-draw wins together all season. Nakashima, fresh from a third round at Roland Garros, is having the better year by a distance and moves through this grass swing with a confidence Struff cannot currently muster. Form over history, at 1.28.

Mirra Andreeva draws the eye-catching assignment: beat a former champion. Barbora Krejcikova lifted this title in 2024, and one does not say that lightly — but she has not beaten a top-10 player since the WTA Finals of that same year, a drought that tells its own story. Andreeva has converted both of her previous Wimbledon second rounds into a last-16 or better (2023 and 2025), has won multiple matches at nine of her thirteen events in 2026, and reached three finals on the clay earlier this summer. The teenager can see off the old champion here without dropping a set. 1.45.

And Belinda Bencic to dismiss Xinyu Wang. The Swiss produced her best-ever Wimbledon last year, beating Mirra Andreeva and Ekaterina Alexandrova on the way to the semi-finals, and she is 15-3 against players outside the top 20 this season — entirely in keeping with the 29-6 she posted a year ago. Wang, by contrast, is 5-7 in Grand Slam second rounds and just 1-2 of them at Wimbledon. Too much grass, too much pedigree, one direction only. 1.31.

The two we ask only for a set

The floor of the slip is deliberately gentle. Roman Safiullin is one of the most unpredictable men on tour — capable of a masterclass or a no-show on any given afternoon — so against Botic van de Zandschulp we do not ask him to win; we ask him to take a single set, priced at 1.12. It helps that he is 2-0 in Wimbledon second rounds and looks to have rediscovered his level, while van de Zandschulp has reached the third round here just once in five visits and stumbled into the Championships badly short of rhythm. Likewise Katerina Siniakova against her young compatriot Nikola Bartunkova: five grass-court quarter-finals speak to real pedigree, but a run of five straight losses to top-50 opposition argues for prudence, so once again we bank only the set at 1.22 rather than the outright.

⚡ Fun fact · just not against each other
Twelve months ago, Belinda Bencic knocked Mirra Andreeva out of Wimbledon on her run to the semi-finals. This Wednesday the slip backs both of them to win — which works nicely, right up until the day the draw sends them at one another again.
Seven names, one surface, one unglamorous thesis: the better player, in their element, tends to win the tennis match. Five to do exactly that, two merely to hold a set. Nothing here is brave — which, as always, is the precise moment the tennis remembers it owes nobody anything.
