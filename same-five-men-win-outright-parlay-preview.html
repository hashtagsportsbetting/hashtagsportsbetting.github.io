Same five men. Different question. The companion to this slip asks each of them only to take a set; this one asks them to take the match. No handicap, no insurance — five clean wins, or nothing. At 7.97, ten euro and change becomes a shade over eighty. Greedier odds, identical logic, considerably worse for the nerves.

Lead with the serve, because on grass the serve leads. Giovanni Mpetshi Perricard is six-foot-eight of first delivery, the closest thing the tour has to a young John Isner, and on a lawn his one trick is the only one that matters. He has held 96% of his service games on grass this season, made the fourth round on his Wimbledon debut, and pushed Taylor Fritz to five sets here a year ago. Yannick Hanfmann serves big too, but his grass form has wobbled, and in a shoot-out of two servers the bookmakers side firmly with the Frenchman. To win outright he need only nick the tie-breaks his serve keeps engineering — which is rather his whole career.

Then the mismatch the ranking hides. Tomas Martin Etcheverry sits thirty places above Lorenzo Sonego and it counts for nothing on a lawn. The Argentine is a clay-court slider with a 5-14 career record on grass who has lost both his warm-up matches this June; Sonego is a serve-and-forehand grass specialist with two Wimbledon fourth rounds and a winning record on the surface. The market, ignoring the rankings entirely, makes the Italian the favourite — and for the outright I am simply siding with the market and the surface against a man who looks lost the moment the bounce stays low.

Two more grass-comfortable favourites carry the middle of the slip. Mackenzie McDonald qualified his way in, but he owns a grass-built forehand and a Wimbledon fourth round from 2018, and his compatriot Patrick Kypson owns neither; the American is the rightful favourite in an all-American opener. Tallon Griekspoor, meanwhile, brings a winning career grass record and a clear price advantage over James Duckworth — an honest Australian veteran, but one whose grass form this season reads a thin 1-3. Both are favoured; both should win; neither requires a leap of faith.

And the anchor, on the only red court of the five. Marco Cecchinato has gone home to Milan to defend the Aspria Tennis Cup, a Challenger he has won twice on the clay where he spent a whole career — a former world No. 16 and 2018 Roland Garros semi-finalist drawing a young wild-card compatriot, Filippo Romano. For the handicap slip he only had to take a set; here, the teacher is asked to win the lesson outright. On his own dirt, against a boy making his way, that is the leg I worry about least.

⚡ Fun fact · a slider, not a gardener
Tomas Martin Etcheverry is ranked No. 32 in the world and carries a perfectly respectable record on clay. On grass, the career ledger reads 5 wins and 14 losses — and this June he has lost both his warm-up matches, including one at Eastbourne in which he actually led by a set before unravelling. He is a slider, and a lawn is the one place a slider has nowhere to slide. Hence Sonego, ranked forty places lower, as the favourite.
Five men, five outright wins, two surfaces, one far braver ask. The serve-bot to nick his tie-breaks, the grass Italian to expose a clay one, McDonald and Griekspoor to bank their edges, and the professor to win his own lesson in Milan. The set-handicap version of this slip is the sensible one. This is the version you keep quiet about — until it lands.
